<section id="glossing-conventions" class="level2">
<h2>Glossing conventions</h2>
<p>While the coding conventions in our corpus basically follow the <a href="http://www.eva.mpg.de/lingua/resources/glossing-rules.php">Leipzig Glossing Rules</a>, we have alternative treatment on Rule 6, 7 and 10. To well capture and represent language specificity of Formosan languages, some other coding conventions are also adopted. Detailed explanations are provided below.</p>
<ol type="A">
<li><p>Coding for inherent categories (Rule 7) is not used.</p></li>
<li><p>The symbol [ ] or ÃƒËœ (Rule 6) is not used for glossing non-overt elements. For example, <em>‘akoy* (Saisiyat) is glossed as ’AF.many.’ Due to the unique morphological structure of focus in Tsou, non-overt elements, such as </em>bonu* 'to eat' are glossed like this: eat.AF. ( See the detailed explanation below in (E).)</p></li>
<li><p>Coding for reduplication is not marked by a tilde, ~ (Rule 10). The three reduplication rules adopted in this corpus are listed as follows.</p>
<ol type="i">
<li>Ca reduplication will be glossed Ca. For example, ha-hila (Saisiyat) is glossed Ca-sun.</li>
<li>Prefix-type reduplication: if the reduplicated portion is at word-initial position, then it is glossed <span class="sc">red</span>-. For example, m-li-lizaq (Kavalan) is glossed: <span class="sc">af</span>-<span class="sc">red</span>-happy.</li>
<li>Infix-type reduplication: a &lt;&gt; symbol is used if the reduplicated portion is at medial position of a word. For example, sa-ru&lt;mi'a&gt;mi'ad (Amis) is glossed sa-&lt;<span class="sc">red</span>&gt;day.</li>
</ol></li>
<li><p>The abbreviations <span class="sc">af</span>, <span class="sc">pf</span>, <span class="sc">rf</span> (<span class="sc">if</span>) and <span class="sc">lf</span> are used to represent Agent Focus, Patient Focus, Referential Focus (Instrumental Focus), and Locative Focus.</p></li>
<li><p>Tsou has different focus morphology from other Formosan languages. The distinction between the AF and PF markers is in the verbal suffix; therefore, the focus markers are glossed after the verb stem. For example, <em>bonU/ana</em> 'to eat' is glossed as 'eat.AF/eat.PF' and <em>boacU/eoeca</em> 'to bite' as 'bite.AF/bite.PF.' A few verbs have <em>-m-</em> or <em>m-</em> forms to mark AF, as opposed to the PF verbs, whose markers are mostly suffixed, for example, <em>tmoecU/teoca</em> 'to hack', <em>matvo'ho/patvo'ha</em> 'to dare to say', and <em>mooteo/totea</em> 'to wait.' For such AF instances, the glossing of focus markers after the verb stem is retained: 'hack.AF/hack.PF', 'dare.to.say.AF/dare.to.say.PF', and 'wait.AF/wait.PF.</p></li>
<li><p>Different abbreviations from the <a href="http://www.eva.mpg.de/lingua/resources/glossing-rules.php">Leipzig Glossing Rules</a> include:</p>
<ol type="i">
<li>The abbreviation <span class="sc">caus</span> is replaced by <span class="sc">cau</span>.</li>
<li>The abbreviation <span class="sc">dem</span> is replaced by 'this' and 'that'.</li>
<li>The abbreviation <span class="sc">nmlz</span> is replaced by <span class="sc">nmz</span>.</li>
<li>The abbreviation <span class="sc">recp</span> is replaced by <span class="sc">rec</span>.</li>
</ol></li>
</ol>
</section>
<section id="discourse-coding" class="level2">
<h2>Discourse coding</h2>
<p>Discourse markings generally follow DuBois, with the following modifications:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li>Truncated intonation units are not marked.</li>
<li>To avoid confusion with the Leipzig Glossing Rules, truncated words are marked -- instead of -.</li>
<li>To avoid possible confusion with the Leipzig Glossing Rules, lengthening is marked == instead of =.</li>
<li>The symbol ‘^,’ originally used for primary accent, is now reserved to indicate a change of intensity, which is perceived as a focused (or "emphasized") prominent syllable which sometimes does not coincide with the stressed pattern of a word.</li>
</ol>
